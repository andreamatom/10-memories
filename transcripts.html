01
Meine Mutter ist eine Ermöglicherin,
My Mom Is An Enabler
Kimberly Duck
GERMAN

TRANSCRIPT

Als ich klein war, gab es im Büro meiner Eltern eine Wand Regal voller Bücher. In den untersten Reihen waren Kinderbücher für uns bereitgestellt. Ich war damals regelrecht besessen von Märchenbüchern und zog diese mit Vorliebe aus dem Regal. Eins dieser Bücher hielt ich mit voller Überzeugung für magisch. Jedes Mal, wenn ich es zurück ins Regal stellte und am nächsten Tag wieder danach griff, war es ein neues Märchen. Dieser Wechsel setzte sich so lange fort, bis die Geschichten schließlich rotierten. Erst später stellte sich heraus, dass meine Mutter die Bücher heimlich austauschte und sich dabei köstlich amüsierte, während ich fest davon überzeugt war, dass ich ein wahrhaft magisches Buch in Händen hielt.

When I was little, my parents' office had a wall of shelves full of books. The bottom rows were filled with children's books for us. I was obsessed with fairy tale books and loved to pull them off the shelf. I was convinced that one of these books was magical. Every time I put it back on the shelf and picked it up again the next day, it was a new fairy tale. This continued until the stories were rotating. It was only later that I discovered that my mother secretly swapped the books and had a lot of fun doing it, while I was convinced that I was holding a truly magical book in my hands.

02
Pfungwa dzeGymnast Mudiki,
The Mind of a Young Gymnast
Tanaka Mapondera
Shona

TRANSCRIPT

Pandaiva mwana mudiki, ndaiva mutimhu yemagymnastics yenyika yeZimbabwe. Nguva yemakwikwi nedzimwe nyika yaindinzwisa mudumbu chaizvo, asi zvainakidza pose pandaitamba zvakanaka ndichikunda vamwe. Mumwe wemakwikwi wandisingakanganwe wakaitirwa munyika yeNamibia pandaiva nemakore gumi nerimwe. Zvaityisa nekuti vamwe vandaikwikwidzana navo vekunedzimwe nyika vagonesesa. Zvisinei hazvo, ndakanakidzwa nenguva yandakafamba kuenda kunyika yeNamibia. Ndinoyeuka nguva iyioyo nemefaro mumoyo mangu.

When I was a little girl, I was in the Zimbabwe gymnastics national team. International competitions often made me really nervous, but I really enjoyed them when I performed well and got podium placements. One of the competitions I'll never forget happened in Namibia when I was 11 years old. It was daunting because the other girls I was competing with from various countries were really good. Regardless of all this, I had a really fun and wholesome time during my travels to Namibia. Reminiscing on this memory really warms my heart.

03
네이트 버튼,
Nate Button
Thea Kim
Korean

TRANSCRIPT

초등학교 6학년 때, 한국에 온 지 반년쯤 되었던 나는 롤리팝이라는 핑크색 휴대폰을 들고 다녔어. 그런데 어느 날, 갑자기 휴대폰이 정지되었다는 안내를 들었지. 알고 보니, 인터넷 접속 버튼인 ‘네이트 버튼’을 실수로 눌러 몇 달간 만화를 본 탓에 요금이 200만 원이나 나온 거야. 이 버튼은 누르기만 해도 인터넷 요금이 폭등하는 위험한 버튼이었는데, 그걸 모르고 계속 썼던 거지. 더 웃긴 건, 이 기술을 개발한 사람이 바로 우리 아빠였다는 거야! 결국 돈을 직접 갚으라는 아빠의 말에 명절 세뱃돈을 모으고, 집안일을 하며 2년 동안 열심히 갚았어. 그 사건은 나를 철들게 하고 돈의 가치를 깨닫게 해준 잊을 수 없는 경험이었어.

You heard me. As it turned out, I had been watching cartoons for months by pressing the ‘Nate button’, the internet connection button, and my bill had reached 2 million won. This button was one that everyone was wary of because their internet bills would skyrocket if they pressed it, but I didn’t know that and kept using it. What’s even funnier is that my dad developed this technology! In the end, I paid it back myself, so I saved up my holiday allowance, ran errands, and got my ping pong score written off by 1,000 won each, and I paid it back diligently for two years. That incident was an unforgettable experience that made me mature and realize the value of money.

04
પડીકુ,
Padiku
Saachi Mehta
GUJARATI

TRANSCRIPT

સુન્દય સવાર નો નાસ્તો, સુન્દય સવાર નું રોઉતીને. હું પાંચ વરસ ની હોઇ, હું હાર સુન્દય સવારે દાદા સાથે ગાંઠિયા લેવા જતી હતી. એ ઊકલે ની દુકાન મારા ઘરે થી પાંચ મિનિટે દૂર હતી, અને જતા જતા દાદા મને કહાની કહતા હતા. હવે મને એ સ્ટોરીઝ બરાબર યાદ નથી, પણ મને યાદ છે કે મને એ સ્ટોરીઝ બહુ ગમતી હતી.

મને એ ઊકલે ની દુકાન હજી પણ યાદ છે. હું એમના કાઉન્ટર સુધી પહોંચતી નહોતી શકતી, પણ મને ગાંઠિયા અને જલેબી ની સુગંધ આવતી હતી. એ ઊકલે હાર ટાઈમે મને મારું પોતાનું પડીકું આપતા, હંમેશા ભાવનગરી ગાંઠિયા, જે મને ખૂબ જ ગમતું હતું, કેમ કે એ મારો પોતાનો નાસ્તો હતો. ઘર પહોંચીને, મમ્મી, પપ્પા, દાદા અને દાદી જલેબી, ગાંઠિયા અને ચા ખાતા હતા, અને હું મારા ભાવનગરી ગાંઠિયા સાથે ચા અને બિસ્કિટ ખાવતી હતી. હાર સુન્દય સવારે અમે એજ બ્રેકફાસ્ટ કરતા હતા, અને એ મારું ફેવરિટ સમય હતો.

Sunday morning breakfast routine. I must have been 5 years old; every Sunday morning, I used to go to buy gathiya with my grandfather. We bought them from an uncle's shop—I remember the uncle so clearly, but I don't remember the name of his shop at all. It was a five-minute walk from my house, and on the way, my grandfather would tell me stories. I don't remember what those stories were about anymore, but I remember enjoying them and looking forward to them.

I remember the uncle's shop. I was tiny and couldn't reach the counter, but I remember the distinct smell of jalebi and fafda. The uncle always gave me my own little packet of bhavnagri gathiya, which was my favorite part of the week because it meant that it was my own little breakfast. We used to have Sunday breakfasts together on the floor of our kitchen—me, my parents, and my grandparents—and it was always jalebi, fafda, and chai. It's one of the most special memories I have of my childhood, and I still think about that time when I have jalebi and fafda for breakfast sometimes.

Неприятное воспоминание из детсада,
An Unpleasant Memory from Kindergarten
Tonia Zhang
Russian

TRANSCRIPT

Мне было лет пять, может, четыре. Моя лучшая подруга в детском саду, Кристина, подарила мне браслет. Господи, как же мне нравился тот браслет. Он был простой, но такой красивый — просто пластмассовый бриллиант на веревке. Однажды, спустя некоторое время, одна популярная девочка в нашем классе (я помню, что ее звали Саша) заметила мой браслет и предложила обмен. Она сказала, что у нее тоже есть красивый камешек, но в форме сердца. Я ее боялась и согласилась на обмен, не понимая, на что я соглашалась. В итоге она просто сняла со своей футболки маленькую наклейку-стразу и дала ее мне. Почему-то в тот момент я ничего не могла ответить, и у меня не нашлось отваги попросить свой браслет обратно. Я до сих пор об этом жалею.

I was about five years old, maybe four. My best friend in kindergarten, Kristina, gave me a bracelet. Oh God, how much I loved that bracelet. It was simple but so beautiful—just a plastic diamond on a string. One day, some time later, a popular girl in our class (I remember her name was Sasha) noticed my bracelet and proposed a trade. She said she also had a beautiful gem, but in the shape of a heart. I was afraid of her, so I agreed to the trade, not really understanding what I was agreeing to. In the end, she just took a small sticker gem off her T-shirt and gave it to me. For some reason, I couldn’t say anything in that moment, and I didn’t have the courage to ask for my bracelet back. I still regret it to this day.

06
Auto de Payasos,
Clown Car
Florencia Villa
Spanish

TRANSCRIPT

Mis primos Juan y Rocío vivían con mis tíos en Arizona, San Luis, a unos 800km de la ciudad de Buenos Aires, donde vivía mi familia. De chiquitos, cuando venían a visitarnos, mis papas solían organizar salidas al shopping mall e íbamos todos juntos en un solo auto. No se por qué, no creo que haya una razón exactamente, pero de alguna manera hacíamos entrar 8 personas en un auto. Le decíamos el auto de los payasos, una vez que estacionábamos, empezaban a salir niños y adultos por las 4 puertas y el baúl del auto también. Recuerdo como peleábamos con mi hermanita y mis primos para ver quienes iban a viajar en el baúl! El lugar más incomodo, más oscuro, pequeño, y más peligroso, era el más codiciado por los niños! Creo que gracias a la terapia inversiva del auto de los payasos ninguno de nosotros salió claustrofóbico…

My cousins ​​Juan and Rocio lived with my uncles in Arizona, San Luis, about 800km from the city of Buenos Aires, where my family lived. When we were little, when they came to visit us, my parents used to organize trips to the shopping mall and we would all go together in one car. I don’t know why, I don’t think there is an exact reason, but somehow we would fit 8 people in one car. We called it the clown car, once we parked, children and adults would start getting out through the 4 doors and the trunk of the car as well. I remember how we would fight with my little sister and my cousins ​​to see who would travel in the trunk! The most uncomfortable, darkest, smallest, and most dangerous place was the most coveted by the children! I think that thanks to the immersive therapy of the clown car none of us became claustrophobic…

T夜里骑车卖馄饨的人,
The Street Merchant That Sells Wonton at Night
Jo Zixuan
Mandarin

TRANSCRIPT

在我的老家会有骑着三轮车的人在夜里卖馄饨。他们会一边骑车一边敲竹杠，提醒附近的人，他们正在卖馄饨。我总是很喜欢吃那种馄饨，然后我爸爸会从窗口探出头叫住卖馄饨的人“诶，馄饨，这里要一碗馄饨。”然后那个车子会停在我们家楼下给我做一碗馄饨。我会看着他从头到尾做馄饨的全过程。

In my hometown there are people on tricycles selling wontons at night. They would rip off people while riding their bikes, alerting people nearby that they were selling wontons. I always like to eat those kinds of wontons, and then my dad will stick his head out of the window and call to the wonton seller, "Hey, wontons, I want a bowl of wontons here." Then the car will park downstairs in our house and make them for me. A bowl of wontons. I would watch him make the wontons from start to finish.

08
Na Komandosa,
On the Commando
Emilia Miękisz
Polish

TRANSCRIPT

Odkąd pamiętam mama zawsze podcinała, goliła włosy tacie. Brała golarkę, stawiała krzesło w salonie i robiła go na jeżyka. Tata zawsze żartował, udawał przy mnie, że go boli, a potem mówił, że wygląda jak komandos. Nazwał to strzyżenie na komandosa. Szukając ostatnio jakiegoś zdjęcia z dzieciństwa znalazłam Taty portret, który zrobiłam jak byłam mała. Nie pamięta jak go robiłam, ale sądząc po jego włosach było to właśnie po jego strzyżeniu na “komandosa” gdyż przykleiłam jego prawdziwe włosy. Teraz jak o tym myślę, to właściwie te chwile wygłupiania się, robienia min wpłynęły na moje własne poczucie humoru i jak wykorzystuje humor w moich ilustracjach i animacjach. Ostatnio nawet znajoma rodziców powiedziała, że widzi mojej sztuce mojego tatę, jego poczucie humoru. Jak widać mój portret z dzieciństwa potwierdza, że jest moją inspiracje. Do dzisiaj się wygłupiamy, a zabawa stała się nie zbędna w mojej praktyce.

For as long as I can remember, Mom always trimmed and shaved Dad's hair. She’d take the razor, set up a chair in the living room, and give him a buzz cut. Dad always joked around, pretending it hurt, and then said he looked like a commando. He called that haircut the "commando cut." While recently looking for some childhood photos, I found a portrait of Dad that I had made when I was little. I don’t remember making it, but judging by his hair, it was right after one of those "commando cuts" because I had glued on some of his actual hair. Now that I think about it, those moments of goofing around and pulling funny faces have shaped my own sense of humor and how I use humor in my illustrations and animations. Just recently, a family friend even said she could see my dad’s humor in my art. As you can see, that childhood portrait proves he’s my inspiration. We still joke around to this day, and playfulness has become essential to my creative practice.

09
Cachorros e Sonhos,
Dogs and Dreams
Barbara Cutlak
Portuguese

TRANSCRIPT

Sou filha única e sempre quis ter um irmão ou irmã. Pedi muito para os meus pais, e, vendo que não ia conseguir, passei a pedir um cachorrinho. Só que minha mãe tinha pavor de animais domésticos e ela que mandava em casa… ou seja, nunca ia conseguir! Mas eu sou persistente e passei a ficar obcecada pelo assunto cachorro. Estudei várias as raças, sabia as características, os prós e contras, comprava revistas sobre cachorro. Ficava louca quando via um cachorro na rua. Segui essa estratégia por uns dois, até chegar no ponto de ter um cachorro imaginário. Não sei como consegui convencer meus pais, mas um dia minha mãe falou: tá bom. Vamos atrás desse cachorro. Foi um dos dias mais feliz que vivi! Eu tinha 12 anos.

I am an only child and I always wanted to have a brother or sister. I asked my parents a lot, and when I saw that I wasn't going to get one, I started asking for a puppy. But my mother was terrified of pets and she was the one in charge at home... in other words, I was never going to get one! But I am persistent and I became obsessed with the subject of dogs. I studied several breeds, I knew their characteristics, pros and cons, I bought magazines about dogs. I went crazy when I saw a dog on the street. I followed this strategy for about two years, until I got to the point of having an imaginary dog. I don't know how I managed to convince my parents, but one day my mother said: OK. Let's go get this dog. It was one of the happiest days of my life! I was 12 years old.

10
Hating Papel,
1 whole sheet of paper
Clara Cayosa
Tagalog

TRANSCRIPT

Bibong bibo ako na batang estudyante, at sa marami, mabuti ito na katangian. Ngunit nung lumipat ako ng eskwelahan pang high school, tinignan ako bilang banta at inisaan ako ng mga bago kong kaklase. Naasar daw sila sa akin. Hangga’t nakarating sa teacher advisor namin at naisip ng teacher magpasa ng papel na hinati pahaba - isang side para isulat ng mga kaklase kung anong gusto nila tungkol sakin, at sa kabila anong ayaw nila. Pinasa itong papel sa buong klase, habang ako’y pinalabas ng klasrum, nakatayo, naghihintay. Pagkatapos ay binasa ng teacher yung mga nakasulat sa papel. Naaalala ko pa yung itsura nung papeles – yung isang side, onti, yung kabila, puno. Para sa isang doce años, traumatizing siya. Kahit na nais kong kalimutan, nagiwan ito ng malaki na tuldok sa buhay ko. Iniisip ko nalang na sa kahit anumang grupo, wala dapat isang nainiiwan.

I was a shy, talkative young student, and to many, this was a good quality. But when I transferred to high school, my new classmates looked at me as a threat and ostracized me. They said they were annoyed with me. Until we got to our teacher advisor and the teacher thought of passing out a piece of paper that was divided lengthwise - one side for the classmates to write what they liked about me, and on the other side what they didn't like. The paper was passed around to the entire class, while I was sent out of the classroom, standing, waiting. Then the teacher read the writing on the paper. I still remember the look of the paper - one side, empty, the other, full. For a twelve-year old, it was traumatizing. Even though I wanted to forget it, it left a big mark on my life. I just think that in any group, no one should be left out.
